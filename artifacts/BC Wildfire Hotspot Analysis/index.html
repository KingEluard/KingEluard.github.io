BC Wildfire Hotspot Analysis

Multi-Year Analysis of Wildfire Activity and Environmental Factors in British Columbia Using R Analytics

Project Timeline: 2024
Academic Project - Langara College
Tools: R, RStudio, Exploratory Analytics

Project Overview

This comprehensive wildfire analysis examines the relationship between wildfire activity and environmental factors across British Columbia over multiple years. Using advanced R programming techniques including chi-square testing, correlation analysis, and spatial data processing with the sf package, we identified regional fire risk patterns and applied statistical methods to understand wildfire behavior and environmental correlations through the tidyverse ecosystem.

Key Objectives

  • Identify regional wildfire hotspots across British Columbia
  • Analyze temporal patterns in wildfire activity over multiple years
  • Examine relationships between environmental factors and fire occurrence
  • Apply exploratory data analysis techniques for pattern recognition
  • Create comprehensive visualizations for trend analysis
  • Develop insights for fire risk assessment and prevention

Key Findings & Insights

Regional Fire Hotspots

Identified distinct wildfire hotspots in the Interior regions of BC, particularly in the Cariboo and Kamloops areas, with significantly higher fire frequency and intensity compared to coastal regions.

Seasonal Patterns

Strong seasonal correlations revealed peak fire activity during July-August, with early spring conditions serving as key predictive indicators for fire season severity.

Environmental Correlations

Temperature and humidity levels showed strong negative correlation with fire risk, while wind patterns and drought indices emerged as significant predictive factors.

Topographical Influence

Elevation and slope analysis revealed increased fire susceptibility in mid-elevation forests (800-1500m), with aspect and vegetation type playing crucial roles.

Human vs Natural Causes

Lightning-caused fires showed different spatial and temporal patterns compared to human-caused fires, with natural fires more prevalent in remote areas during peak electrical storm seasons.

Climate Change Indicators

Analysis revealed increasing fire frequency and intensity trends over the study period, consistent with regional climate change projections and warming temperatures.
